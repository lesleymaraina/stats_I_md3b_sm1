From Single-Group Estimation to Comparative Estimation

In the previous section, we discussed how estimation works for a single population quantity:

  • point estimates for one mean or one proportion
  • sampling variability and standard error
  • confidence intervals for a single parameter

In the current lecture we will focus on comparative estimation

How different are two groups, and how should that difference be estimated and interpreted?

Biomedical examples: clinical trials, comparative effectiveness studies, and treatment evaluations are designed to estimate between-group effects

Estimation for Comparing Groups

Central Goal: discuss statistical estimation for group comparisons

Discuss the following comparative estimands:

  • difference in sample means for a continuous outcome
  • risk difference for a binary outcome
  • risk ratio for a binary outcome
  • odds and odds ratios for a binary outcome

Each comparative estimand follows the same general framework:

  • estimate the outcome within each group
  • compare groups on an interpretable scale
  • use confidence intervals to judge both magnitude and precision

Review: Statistical Estimation

  • statistical estimation involves turning observed sample data into evidence about an unknown population quantity

  • for estimand comparisons, estimation answers a question like this:

based on the differences observed in the study sample, what is the best estimate of the corresponding treatment effect in the population?

Process inovlves the following:

  1. define the population quantity of interest
  2. calculate a sample-based quantity that targets it
  3. interpret that quantity as an estimate of the underlying effect

Estimating Differences Between Groups

  • goal is to understand how two groups differ:

  • treatment vs control

  • exposed vs unexposed

  • biomarker-positive vs biomarker-negative

We are now estimating:

a contrast between two population quantities

Defining the Estimand in Group Comparisons

Review:

  • estimand [what we want to learn] : population quantity we aim to learn about

  • estimator [method used to estimate target]: rule or formula used to approximate the estimand using sample data

In a two-group comparison, the estimand shows the relationship between two population quantities, such as:

  • difference in population means
  • difference in population probabilities
  • ratio of population probabilities
  • ratio of population odds

Defining How Groups Differ: Multiple Comparative Measures

In a two-group study, the data provide:

  • an outcome in the treatment group
  • an outcome in the control group

From these, we can estimate how the groups differ

But there is not just one way to define that difference.

Differences between groups [treatment effect] can be framed as:

  • absolute change in outcome
  • relative change in outcome
  • comparison of event vs non-event likelihood

Each of which defines a different population quantity [estimand] that the study is trying to estimate

Defining How Groups Differ: Multiple Comparative Measures

Multiple comparative measures can exist within a study:

  • mean difference → estimates difference in population averages
  • risk difference → estimates absolute difference in probabilities
  • risk ratio → estimates relative difference in probabilities
  • odds ratio → estimates difference on the odds scale

What changes is:

the question we are asking about how the groups differ in the population

The Structure of All Comparative Estimators

comparative measures follow a similar estimation structure:

  1. estimate the outcome in each group
  2. combine those estimates into a comparison
  3. interpret that comparison as a population effect

Mean Difference

\[ \widehat{\Delta} = \bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_0 \]

Mean Difference:

  • mean difference is the difference between the average outcome in two groups.

  • \(\bar{x}_1\): sample mean in the treatment group

  • \(\bar{x}_0\): sample mean in the control group

quantity is used to estimate the corresponding population mean difference:\(\mu_1 - \mu_0\) - true difference in average outcome between the two groups in the full population

estimator of the population mean difference: \(\mu_1 - \mu_0\)

Risk Difference

\[ \widehat{RD} = \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_0 \]

risk difference : sample-based estimate of the true absolute difference in event probability between groups in the population

For a binary outcome, each group has a population event probability:

  • \(p_1\): true probability of the event in the treatment group population
  • \(p_0\): true probability of the event in the control group population

study provides sample-based estimates because the population probabilities are unknown because the full population is not observed:

  • \(\hat{p}_1\): observed event proportion in the treatment group
  • \(\hat{p}_0\): observed event proportion in the control group

Risk Difference

\[ \widehat{RD} = \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_0 \]

Risk difference compares these two probabilities and asks:

how many probability units apart are the groups?

Therefore

\[ \widehat{RD} = \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_0 \]

is used to estimate:

\(RD = p_1 - p_0\)

the true difference in event probability between treatment and control in the population

Risk Difference

Example:

Consider a clinical trial comparing a new therapy to standard care, where the outcome is treatment response (yes/no)

Suppose the observed response probabilities in the sample are:

  • treatment group: \(\hat{p}_1 = 0.62\)
  • control group: \(\hat{p}_0 = 0.48\)

if the treatment response probability is 0.62 and the control response probability is 0.48, then the estimand risk difference is:

\[ \widehat{RD} = 0.62 - 0.48 = 0.14 \]

Interpretation: there is an estimated 14 percentage-point increase in response probability under treatment

Risk Ratio

\[ \widehat{RR} = \frac{\hat{p}_1}{\hat{p}_0} \]

risk ratio :

  • estimator of the population relative risk
  • quantifies how many times larger [or smaller] the outcome probability is in one group compared to another

risk ratio compares two estimated probabilities:

  • numerator: \(\hat{p}_1\)
    → outcome probability for the treatment group → e.x.: estimated probability of the outcome in the treatment group

  • denominator: \(\hat{p}_0\)
    → outcome probability for the control group → e.x.: estimated probability of the outcome in the control group

Risk Ratio

\[ \widehat{RR} = \frac{\hat{p}_1}{\hat{p}_0} \]

Question:

how many times as likely is the outcome in the treatment group compared to the control group?

Interpretation

  • \(\widehat{RR} = 1\) → equal probabilities
  • \(\widehat{RR} > 1\) → outcome more likely in treatment
  • \(\widehat{RR} < 1\) → outcome less likely in treatment

Odds and Odds Ratio

\[ \widehat{OR} = \frac{\hat{p}_1/(1-\hat{p}_1)}{\hat{p}_0/(1-\hat{p}_0)} \]

odds ratio

  • metric measures the association between two groups by comparing their odds
  • sample-based estimate of how the odds of an outcome differ between groups in the population [expressed on an odds scale]

Note:

  • probabilities describe frequency of events
  • odds describe event relative to non-event

Important distinction

  • probability (\(p\)): proportion of individuals who experience the event
  • odds (\(p/(1-p)\)): ratio of event probability to non-event probability

Comparing Groups Using Different Measures

Each measure estimates a different type of population comparision between groups, and answer:

how do outcomes differ between groups in the population?

Where:

  • Mean difference → difference in averages
  • Risk difference → difference in probabilities
  • Risk ratio → relative probability
  • Odds ratio → relative odds

Clinical Trial Context: Defining the Treatment Effect

Consider a randomized clinical study evaluating a new therapy compared to standard care

Study Goal: estimate how the treatment changes those outcomes in the target population

Key outcomes of interest include:

  • Clinical response [binary]: did the patient respond to treatment?
  • Serious adverse events [binary]: did the patient experience a harmful event?
  • Change in kidney function (eGFR) [continuous]: how much did kidney function improve or decline?

Connection to estimation

  • outcomes are measured on different scales, the treatment effect must be defined and estimated differently:

  • mean difference : estimates difference in average eGFR change

  • risk difference : estimates absolute difference in probability (response or adverse event)

  • risk ratio : estimates relative difference in probability

  • odds ratio : estimates difference on the odds scale

Difference in Sample Means

\[ \widehat{\Delta} = \bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_0 \]

estimated mean difference between two groups

  • \(\widehat{\Delta}\) : estimated population mean difference
    • difference in eGFR change 
  • \(\bar{x}_1\) : sample mean in group 1
    • sample mean eGFR change in the new therapy group
  • \(\bar{x}_0\) : sample mean in group 0
    • sample mean eGFR change in the standard care group
  • positive value : average outcome is higher in group 1
  • negative value : average outcome is lower in group 1

estimated difference in average change in eGFR between treatment groups

Measure compares:

average change in kidney function for patients receiving the new therapy
vs
average change in kidney function for patients receiving standard care

Estimated Risk Difference for Clinical Response

Goal: find the difference in response probabilities between the two treatment groups

\[ \hat{p}_{new}−\hat{p}_{standard} \]

{new}: proportion of patients who responded under the new therapy {standard}: proportion who responded under standard care

How much does the probability of clinical response change between the two groups?

positive value → higher response probability under the new therapy negative value → lower response probability under the new therapy value near zero → little or no difference between groups

  2. combine those estimates into a comparison
  3. interpret the result as a population effect

  • mean difference → difference in averages (continuous outcomes)
  • risk difference → absolute difference in probabilities
  • risk ratio → relative difference in probabilities
  • odds ratio → relative difference on the odds scale
